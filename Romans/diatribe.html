Romans: A Logical Argument

Understanding the Form and Format of the Epistle

In the epistle, or letter, to the Romans, Paul is using a dialectical format called a Diatribe.

Dialectic: Discovering Truth Through Logical Exchange

In an argument presentation, dialectic is a method of reasoning that seeks to discover truth through the exchange and logical reconciliation of opposing ideas.
It is a process of critical analysis and dialogue (or even a single structured presentation) that aims for a higher, more comprehensive understanding rather than simply "winning" a debate.

Diatribe: A Vigorous, Conversational Moral Discourse

A diatribe (Greek *diatribē*, meaning "pastime" or "discourse") was a form of popular moral discourse or lecture among Greek and Roman philosophers.

It is an oral or written presentation that uses a vigorous, conversational style to explore ethical questions and find truth.
  • **Dialogical Approach:**
    • It often employed a dialogue format with an imaginary or hypothetical opponent (interlocutor).
    • It used a question-and-answer structure, rhetorical questions, and responses to anticipated objections.
    • This was an extension of the Socratic method, designed to guide students or listeners to a better understanding of a philosophical point.
  • **Moral Exhortation:**
    • The primary goal was not simply debate, but moral instruction and persuasion.
    • It aimed to expose contradictions in the audience's presuppositions and help them overcome false and biased ideas they held.
  • **Accessible Style:**
    • The language used is often colloquial, direct, and sometimes informal.
    • It utilized colorful, everyday imagery, anecdotes, and historical examples to make complex philosophical ideas accessible to a general audience.
  • **Focus on a Single Theme:**
    • These discourses typically centered on a single ethical or philosophical theme.
    • Paul’s overall theme is the election of grace, the understanding that the Gentile believers in Christ were as much God’s chosen people and equal part of the Kingdom as Jewish believers, and the implications of salvation by grace through faith for the believer’s life.

The Connective Tissue: Inferential and Explanatory Words

Words like **"For," "Because," "Wherefore,"** and **"Therefore"** are not prepositions; they are **logical connectors** or **inferential markers**. They are essential transitional phrases that structure the debate, allowing the reader to trace Paul's logic from premises to conclusions.

1. Explanatory (Causal) Markers: "For" & "Because"

These introduce the **reason** or **cause** behind a previous statement. They tell the reader, "Here is the evidence that supports what I just said."

2. Inferential (Conclusive) Markers: "Wherefore" & "Therefore"

These introduce the **consequence** or **conclusion** drawn from the preceding facts. They tell the reader, "Based on everything I just proved, this is the logical outcome."

Marker Type Key Words Function in Argument
**Explanatory (Premise)** **For** (*gar*), **Because** Introduces the **reason**, **evidence**, or **bedrock doctrine** that justifies the preceding claim.
**Inferential (Conclusion)** **Therefore** (*dio*, *oun*, *ara*), **Wherefore** Introduces the **logical outcome** or **major implication** of the premises just established.

Paul’s Logical, “If/Then” Structure

Paul’s diatribe uses a logical, “if/then” structure.
He teaches the bedrock doctrines of faith as the building blocks for the main argument.

IF

Everyone (Jew & Gentile) is under sin.

THEN

Salvation is by grace through faith.

THEREFORE

Gentile believers are equal in the Kingdom.

HOW THEN

Should believers live?
