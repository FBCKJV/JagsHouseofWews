The Meaning of "Loved" and "Hated"

In Scripture, "loved" and "hated" often mean preferred over or chosen above the other, not absolute personal hatred or despising as we use the words today.

Jesus said: If any man come to me, and hate not his father, and mother, and wife, and children, and brethren, and sisters, yea, and his own life also, he cannot be my disciple (Luke 14:26).

This does not mean literal hatred or despising of family, but to love and prefer Christ more than all others — to place Him first.

In the same way, "I loved Jacob, but Esau have I hated" means God preferred/chose Jacob's line over Esau's — not that He personally despised Esau as an individual.

The Context: Nations, Not Individuals

• The reference is not to the individual twins at birth in Genesis 25.

• It is not about God hating and condemning Esau personally in the womb.

• Esau was reconciled with Jacob, received his own blessing from Isaac, and lived prosperously (Genesis 33; 36).

• The statement concerns the nations that came from them: Israel (Jacob) vs. Edom (Esau).

• Edom (the nation) fell away, refused to help Israel in the wilderness, rejoiced in Judah’s captivity, and was ultimately destroyed ~1400 years later (Obadiah; Malachi 1:4).

• Paul uses this to show: God’s choice of one nation/line over another (by grace, not works) is consistent — just as Gentiles are now grafted in while partial hardness came to unbelieving Jews (Romans 11).

God Judges Individuals by Faith • Nations by Works

• God judges individuals according to faith — whether they believe and call upon the Lord (Romans 10:13; Genesis 4:26).

• Nations are judged here on earth according to their works and collective actions — obedience or rebellion, mercy or enmity (e.g., Edom’s treatment of Israel led to national judgment).

Esau personally was blessed and reconciled; Edom as a nation was judged for its unbelief and hostility.

The same principle applies in Romans: believing Jews and Gentiles are saved by faith; the unbelieving nation faced hardening and partial blindness.

The phrase "Jacob have I loved, but Esau have I hated" refers to God’s preference of one nation over another — not personal hatred of Esau as an individual.

"Loved" and "hated" here mean preferred/chosen above, just as Jesus taught to "hate" family in comparison to loving Him supremely.

Individuals are judged by faith; nations are judged here on earth by their works.

Paul’s point: God’s choice of lines/nations by grace is biblical — consistent with the gospel to the Gentiles and the pattern throughout Scripture.
