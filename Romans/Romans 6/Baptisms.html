Baptism in Romans 6

"Baptized into Christ" • Spiritual Union vs. Water Baptism

The Greek word baptisma means to immerse or become part of by immersion. In the New Testament it does not always refer to water baptism. In Romans 6, Paul describes believers being baptized into Jesus Christ and into his death (vv. 3-4)—a spiritual reality accomplished by the Holy Spirit at the moment of faith, placing us in union with Christ's death, burial, and resurrection. This is the baptism of regeneration (the baptism with fire). It is not referring to water baptism, which is only an outward symbol.

Places believer in Christ instantly upon faith; the real identification that makes us dead to sin and alive to God. A like figure (symbol) of salvation, not the cause (1 Peter 3:21 – “The like figure whereunto even baptism doth also now save us... the answer of a good conscience toward God”).
Happens to all true believers the moment they believe; no delay possible. Can be separated from receiving the Spirit (Acts 8:12-16 – Samaritans baptized in water but later received the Spirit; Acts 10:44-48 – Cornelius’ household received Spirit first, then water baptized).
The baptism with the Holy Ghost and fire that John said Christ would bring (Matt. 3:11); regeneration itself. John preached “the baptism of repentance” (change of mind) to prepare for Christ (Mark 1:4; Acts 19:4).
Paul’s primary mission: the spiritual baptism that unites us to Christ (1 Cor. 1:17 – “Christ sent me not to baptize [in water], but to preach the gospel”). Important ordinance, but secondary to the gospel; Paul distinguished it from his main calling.

Key Insight: Water baptism and the Spirit’s baptism are sometimes separate in Acts, proving they are distinct. The baptism in Romans 6 is the spiritual placement into Christ—the real union that makes us dead to sin and alive to God through the baptism of regeneration by the Holy Spirit.
